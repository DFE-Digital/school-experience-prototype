Help and support candidates with disability and access needs

Read this guidance to find out you can help and support candidates with disability and access needs requirements.

Dealing with requests

Ask all candidates if they have disability or access needs and if they require any adjustments for school experience.

Each individual will be the best 'expert' on their needs. Two people may have the same condition but have different needs and preferences.

If you don't ask people directly if they need any adjustments then some participants won’t be confident enough to ask.

Leading up to school experience

Provide all candidates with an outline of their school experience day and agenda in advance.

It’s good practice to share this information in advance as it helps all candidates - even if they don’t have specific disability or access needs.

However, providing information up front is particularly beneficial for candidates with autistic spectrum disorders and reading disabilities such as anxiety or dyslexia.



Provide all printed material in at least font size 14.

It's good practice as:

  • 50% of the population can't read fonts smaller than 12 point
  • reading is easier and quicker for most people in larger fonts


Allow candidates to bring laptops and digital devices to school experience if they ask.

Candidates with dyslexia might prefer to follow along on their own laptops and devices and may also have their own assistive technology (software) installed on them to help with their reading and comprehension.

Candidates with sight impairments may find it difficult or impossible to use paper materials.



Provide candidates with advance copies of any electronic and printed materials.

This can support candidates with anxiety, autistic spectrum disorders, dyslexia and hearing and visual impairments.



Tell candidates about disabled access, lifts and accessible car parking and toilets.

If you have rooms that are not wheelchair accessible or a 'long walk' away, offer to relocate classes or school experience sessions.

This will be a key concern for candidates with mobility issues and you need to consider candidates who may not be able to walk very far as well as wheelchair users.



Tell candidates about any additional access support you have such as hearing loops and let them know if you have good acoustic sound levels.

If a room has lots of hard surfaces and is ‘echoey’ it may cause problems.

For example, 50% of over 50s have hearing loss and an increasing number of young people are experiencing it as well.

Good communication support tactics, such as 1 person speaking at a time, loops and good acoustics (ie lagging on walls) all support those with hearing impairments.



Let candidates know how to book communication support.

For example, sign language interpreters, speech-to-text services or lip speakers.

Make sure you’re clear on who provides, books and pays for these services.

The recommended lead times for booking BSL interpreters is 6 weeks so you’ll need to build this into your planning.



If candidates want to discuss their access needs allow them to contact you directly before booking school experience.

This is very important for candidates who require communication support or have more significant needs such as anxiety.



Use plain english in all communications.

For example, the use of metaphors can be particularly problematic for people with autistic spectrum disorders.



Ask candidates for their preferred method of contact.

Some candidates may be unable or struggle with certain forms of contact.

For example, candidates with autistic spectrum disorders and hearing impairments may struggle with phone calls.

On the day

Ask candidates where they want to sit.

This is particularly important for candidates with anxiety, autistic spectrum disorders, mobility issues and hearing and visually impairments.



Provide some printed materials in larger fonts for candidates.

We recommend you use an 18-point font printed in landscape because it’s easier to print in landscape for fonts of this size and above.

However, some candidates will even struggle using 14-point fonts and not everyone will be confident enough to ask you to make adjustments.

This means having at least a few copies of materials in larger fonts can be helpful.

It’s good practice to let candidates know that the can ask for larger font printed materials in advance so add this information to your school profile if you offer this service.

On request

If candidates ask, print materials in larger fonts.

Some candidates will have very specific needs. For example, large print means different things to different people and some may want materials printed in a specific font.

It’s good practice to let candidates know that the can ask for larger font printed materials in advance so add this information to your school profile if you offer this service.



If candidate ask, print materials on different colour backgrounds.

This is particularly helpful for candidates with dyslexia as reading black text on a white background can be very difficult and having different colour texts and backgrounds can make reading easier.

Even an off-white background can help candidates and many use yellow paper or other pastel colours.

Candidates with dyslexia may also prefer to receive material electronically and follow along on their own laptop or digital device.
